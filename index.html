01 Research

I did my undergraduate research with Dr. Gareth Roberts in the Cultural Evolution of Language Lab. The group studies how languages evolve through social learning, often in miniature languages built for controlled experiments.

Featured study

Competing Cues in the Emergence of Animacy-Based Grammatical Gender Systems

We taught participants a miniature language in which nouns fell into two classes by animacy. During training, a noun's animate or inanimate meaning pointed to one class while its co-occurrence with class markers pointed to the other. Test trials then introduced unseen nouns and recorded which class participants assigned.

Related work

Prior work on noun-class learning, animacy in miniature languages, and grammar change under social pressure informed the design.

  • Culbertson, Gagliardi & Smith (2017) Competition between phonological and semantic cues in noun class learning

    Semantic and formal cues can conflict during training; learners favor whichever cue they encountered first.

  • Vihman, Nelson & Kirby (2018) Animacy distinctions arise from iterated learning

    Animacy-based noun classes can spread because they make a miniature language easier to transmit.

  • Fedzechkina, Hall Hartley & Roberts (2022) Social biases can lead to less communicatively efficient languages

    From the Cultural Evolution of Language Lab. Learners reshape grammar under competing social and communicative pressures.

  • Culbertson, Gagliardi & Smith (2018) Children's sensitivity to phonological and semantic cues during noun class learning

    The same cue-competition logic shows up in child learners, not just adults.
